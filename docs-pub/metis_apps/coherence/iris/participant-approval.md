# Participant Approval Guide

If you took part in a recorded conversation, you may receive a link asking you to review and
approve what will be published from it before it goes any further. This guide is for you, the
participant. (Staff running the conversation should see [Using IRIS](using-iris.md) instead.)

---

## The approval link

You'll receive a personal link — typically by the channel you were contacted on (for
example, Telegram). The link is unique to you: it opens **your** review page directly, so you
don't need an account or a password to use it. Just open it in a browser.

Approval links expire after a while. If yours says it's expired or invalid, ask the person
who sent it for a fresh one.

## What you can review

Your review page shows what's planned for publication:

- The **hosted video** (uploaded as *unlisted* — only people with the link can see it, it's
  not public yet).
- The **title** and **subtitle**.
- The **thumbnail** image.
- Selected **quotes** from the conversation.
- **Your own LinkedIn post draft** — written from your perspective, for you to share.

You can **edit your LinkedIn draft** right on the page before approving — change the wording
to something you're happy putting out under your name.

## Approving

When you're happy, click **approve**. That records your approval (and saves any edit you made
to your LinkedIn draft).

Your approval is one of the gates the pipeline waits on. Publishing only continues once
**everyone whose approval is required has approved** — so nothing goes out based on your
part until you've said yes. Until then, the conversation simply waits.

## If something looks wrong

If anything is inaccurate, misrepresents you, or you're just not comfortable with it,
**don't approve** — instead reply to whoever sent you the link. Editorial control stays with
the team running the conversation; approval is there precisely so you're not surprised by how
you're represented. Raising a concern holds publishing, because your approval is required for
it to proceed.
